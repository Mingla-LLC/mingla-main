## 1. What Mingla Actually Is

Mingla is a **date and hangout discovery engine** that sorts the world into 13 mood-based categories and 4 price tiers, then lets couples and friend groups plan together in real time.

It is NOT:
- A restaurant recommendation app (Google Maps, Yelp)
- A dating app (Hinge, Bumble)
- A travel planner (Tripadvisor, Wanderlog)
- A social network (Instagram, BeReal)

It IS:
- A **taste engine** that makes you look thoughtful
- A **collaboration tool** that eliminates "idk wbu?" from group chats
- A **novelty machine** that keeps relationships feeling fresh

**One-liner:** Mingla turns "what should we do?" into "let's go."

## 2. User Psychology

### The Core Emotion: Fear of Being Boring

The target user isn't searching for restaurants. They're managing a quiet anxiety: *Am I running out of ideas? Is this relationship becoming routine? Will this person lose interest because I can't plan something better than what we did last week?*

This is **status anxiety disguised as logistics.** They don't lack options — Google has millions. They lack **confidence in their taste.**

### Three Psychological Needs Mingla Serves

| Need | Description | How Mingla Delivers |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Social currency** | "I'm the one who always knows a spot" | Curated, non-obvious places sorted by mood — users look like they did the research |
| **Decision relief** | Paralysis kills excitement before the date even starts | Swipe interface + mood categories = decision in under 60 seconds |
| **Novelty as relationship fuel** | Same places → same conversations → staleness | Continuous discovery of new places keeps the relationship environment changing |

### The Emotional Journey

```
BEFORE MINGLA:
"We should do something" → 30 min group chat debate → "fine let's just
do what we always do" → mediocre night → guilt/boredom

WITH MINGLA:
"We should do something" → open app → swipe → "this looks amazing,
let's go" → great night → "how did you find this place?"
```

That last line — **"how did you find this place?"** — is the moment you're engineering. That's the dopamine hit. That's what makes users come back and tell friends.

## 3. Target Segments

### Primary: Couples (6 months – 3 years)

- **Why this window:** Past the "everything is exciting" honeymoon phase, before the "we've accepted routine" resignation phase. This is when the anxiety peaks.
- **Age:** 23–34
- **Behavior:** Already going on dates 2–4x/month. Already spending $50–$300 per outing. The money isn't the problem — the *ideas* are the problem.
- **Acquisition trigger:** One partner feels the staleness and looks for a solution.

### Secondary: Friend Groups (3–6 people)

- **Why:** Group planning is even harder than couples planning. More opinions = more paralysis.
- **Age:** 22–30
- **Behavior:** Plan group outings 1–2x/month. Heavy group chat users. One "planner" friend does all the work.
- **Acquisition trigger:** The planner friend discovers Mingla and becomes the evangelist.

### On-Ramp: People New to a City

- **Why:** Identical psychology to core user ("I want to discover and look like I know what I'm doing") but with even higher urgency.
- **Retention path:** They become locals. They stay.
- **Acquisition trigger:** Just moved, searching "things to do in [city]" or asking friends for recs.

### Explicitly NOT Targeting: Tourists/Travelers

- Travel is a solved, hypercompetitive space (Google Maps, Tripadvisor, GetYourGuide)
- Tourists want "must-see highlights" — Mingla curates "hidden gems locals love." Different product.
- Tourists don't collaborate the way Mingla enables (ongoing relationships, repeated planning)
- Chasing tourists fractures curation quality and dilutes the brand
- **Revisit in v2** only if retention data shows organic tourist usage

## 5. Pricing Strategy

### Current Model (Problems)

| | Free | Pro | Elite |
|---|---|---|---|
| Swipes | 20/day | Unlimited | Unlimited |
| Sessions | 1 | 3 | Unlimited |
| Pairing | **No** | **No** | Yes |
| Curated cards | No | Yes | Yes |

**Critical problems:**
1. **Pairing is gated behind Elite.** Collaboration is Mingla's moat AND its viral growth mechanism. Gating it kills both retention and word-of-mouth. It's like Spotify locking shared playlists behind Premium.
2. **Three tiers create decision paralysis** for a new, unproven brand. Users don't know enough about you to evaluate Pro vs Elite.
3. **7-day trial creates bait-and-switch psychology.** User experiences full magic → trial ends → best features disappear → feels like punishment, not upgrade.
4. **Free tier is too crippled** (20 swipes, no collab, no curation) to demonstrate value. It's a demo, not a product.

### Recommended Model

**Two tiers. Pairing is free. Upgrade is expansion, not restoration.**

| | Free | Mingla+ |
|---|---|---|
| Swipes | **Unlimited** | Unlimited |
| Pairing | **Yes (up to 2 pairings)** | Unlimited pairings |
| Sessions | 1 active | Unlimited |
| Session members | 3 max | 15 max |
| Curated cards | Browse (can't save) | Full access + save |
| Custom starting point | No | Yes |
| Price tier filters | All | All |

**Why this works:**
- **Free is genuinely useful.** A couple can pair, swipe, and plan a date. They experience the core magic. They tell friends.
- **Upgrade trigger is natural.** "I want to plan with my whole friend group" or "I need more than one session" = expansion, not desperation.
- **No bait-and-switch.** Free stays good forever. Paid adds *more*, doesn't restore what was taken.
- **One decision.** Free or Mingla+. That's it.

### Pricing Tiers

| Period | Price | Effective Monthly | Role |
|--------|-------|-------------------|------|
| **Weekly** | **$1.99/week** | ~$8.57/mo | The "try it this weekend" impulse buy |
| **Monthly** | **$4.99/month** | $4.99/mo | The anchor — what most users land on |
| **Yearly** | **$39.99/year** | $3.33/mo | The lock-in — "Save 33%" |

**Why each period exists:**

**Weekly ($1.99) — The Gateway.** NOT the money-maker — it's a conversion tool. Someone planning a date *this Friday* doesn't want to commit to a month. $1.99 for one weekend is an impulse buy. After 2–3 weeks of paying $1.99, they realize they're overpaying vs monthly — and upgrade. The weekly **trains them to value the product** and makes monthly feel like a deal. If priced too low ($0.99), no incentive to upgrade. If too high ($3.99+), nobody tries it.

**Monthly ($4.99) — The Anchor.** Where most subscribers sit. Below dating app territory ($9.99 Hinge, $17.99 Bumble). Above throwaway territory ($0.99–$2.99). The mental math: the date costs $150, the app costs $5.

**Yearly ($39.99) — The Lock-In.** 33% savings vs monthly ($3.33/mo vs $4.99/mo). Smooths seasonal revenue. Annual subscribers retain 2–3x better than monthly (psychological commitment + forgetting to cancel). $39.99 not $49.99 — at launch, priority is building a loyal base, not maximizing per-user revenue.

### Paywall Anchoring Strategy (Decoy Pricing)

On the paywall screen, show all three with the weekly effective monthly rate visible:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                      │
│  YEARLY          $39.99/yr    ← BEST │
│  $3.33/mo — Save 33%          VALUE  │
│                                      │
│  MONTHLY         $4.99/mo           │
│  Most popular                        │
│                                      │
│  WEEKLY          $1.99/wk           │
│  $8.57/mo effective                  │
│                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The weekly option's *real job* is to make the other two look good. Showing $8.57/mo effective makes monthly look like a bargain. Showing "Save 33%" makes yearly feel smart. Classic decoy pricing — the weekly isn't there to be chosen, it's there to anchor.

### Price Signals to Watch Post-Launch

| Signal | What it means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| >50% choose weekly | Weekly too close to monthly value | Raise to $2.49 or add monthly-exclusive perks |
| <5% choose yearly | Discount not compelling enough | Drop to $34.99/yr (42% savings) or add yearly-only perk |
| Trial→paid <3% | Value prop problem, not price | Fix onboarding before touching price |
| Churn spikes at month 2 | Not enough new value after initial discovery | Pool freshness issue, not pricing |

**Never offer a "lifetime" option.** It caps value of best users forever, attracts deal-seekers not engaged users, and kills recurring revenue story for fundraising. If ever used, it's a limited promo ("first 100 users"), never a permanent tier.

**Anchoring copy:** A couple spending $150 on dinner is paying $4.99 for the curation that *found* that dinner. Frame it as: "Less than a coffee. Better than an hour of Googling."

### Trial Strategy

- **Don't trial on signup.** Let them use Free. Let them love it.
- **Trigger trial when they hit a natural limit:** "You've used your 1 session — want to create another? Try Mingla+ free for 14 days."
- **14 days, not 7.** Seven days isn't enough to form a habit. Fourteen days = two weekends of dates = emotional investment.

### Referral System (Keep, But Restructure)

Current: 1 month Elite per referral. Good mechanic, wrong tier name.

Recommended:
- Referred user gets **14-day Mingla+ trial** (not 7 days)
- Referrer gets **1 month Mingla+ free** per successful referral
- Cap at 6 months banked (prevents gaming)
- The referral IS the collaboration: "Download Mingla so we can plan together" = product action AND marketing action in one sentence

## 6. Marketing & Distribution

### The Foundational Truth

Mingla's growth engine is **not ads.** It's the moment one person in a couple or friend group says: *"Download this app, I'll add you."*

Every marketing dollar should amplify that moment — not replace it.

### Channel Strategy (Ranked by Priority)

#### Tier 1: Organic Social (Cost: Time only)

**Why first:** You need to validate messaging before spending money. Organic tests what resonates.

**TikTok/Instagram Reels (Primary)**
- Couples content is one of the largest categories on both platforms
- Format: 15–30 second skits dramatizing the problem → Mingla as the punchline
- Content pillars:
  - **"The debate"** — couple arguing about where to go, one opens Mingla, solved
  - **"The reaction"** — partner's face when they arrive somewhere amazing ("how did you find this?")
  - **"The upgrade"** — side-by-side of boring routine date vs. Mingla-curated date
  - **"The planner friend"** — one friend always does the work, Mingla as their secret weapon
- Post 4–5x/week. Volume matters more than polish at this stage.
- Use trending sounds. The algorithm rewards relevance over production value.

**Twitter/X (Secondary)**
- Relatable one-liners: "Month 8 of the relationship is just taking turns saying 'I don't know, what do you want to do?'"
- Quote-tweet format drives engagement
- Link in bio → App Store

**Key metric:** Track which content pillar drives the most App Store clicks (use UTM links or branch.io deep links per content type).

#### Tier 2: Micro-Influencer Partnerships (Cost: $200–$1,000/creator)

**Why:** Couples and lifestyle creators (10K–100K followers) have high trust and low cost. They're also actively looking for content ideas.

**The play:**
- Don't pay for a "sponsored post." Pay for a **date night challenge.**
- Brief: "Let Mingla pick your entire date. Film the experience. Be honest."
- The content creates itself — it's a mini-vlog with a built-in narrative arc.
- Creator gets free Mingla+ for a year + flat fee ($200–$500 for 10K–50K creators).

**Why this works:**
- Authentic. The creator is genuinely discovering places, not reading a script.
- Reusable. You can repost their content on your own channels.
- Measurable. Each creator gets a unique referral code → track installs directly.

**Target:** 10–20 creators per launch city. Prioritize:
- Couples content creators
- "Things to do in [city]" accounts
- Food/lifestyle bloggers who cover experiences (not just food photos)

**Avoid:** Mega-influencers (500K+). Expensive, low trust, wrong audience density.

#### Tier 3: SEO/Content (Cost: Time, pays off in 3–6 months)

**Why:** "Things to do in [city]" and "date ideas [city]" are massive search terms with high intent.

**The play:**
- Blog or landing pages: "47 Date Ideas in [City] You Haven't Tried" — each idea links to Mingla category
- City-specific landing pages: mingla.com/cities/[city] — optimized for "[city] date ideas"
- Each page has a clear CTA: "Or skip the list — let Mingla sort it for you."

**Why this works long-term:**
- Evergreen traffic. Someone Googling "date ideas Austin" today will Google it next month too.
- Captures the "new to city" on-ramp segment perfectly.
- Builds domain authority that compounds.

**Start NOW even if traffic is low.** SEO takes months to kick in. Every week you delay is a week of compounding you lose.

#### Tier 4: Venue Partnerships (Cost: $0 — mutual value)

**Why:** Restaurants, bars, and experience venues want foot traffic. You want credibility and content.

**The play:**
- Partner with 10–20 standout venues per launch city
- Offer: "We'll feature you as a Mingla Pick to our users in [city]"
- Ask for: A small perk for Mingla users (10% off, free appetizer, skip the wait)
- Co-branded social content: "This week's Mingla Pick: [Venue]" — both accounts post

**Why this works:**
- Venues validate your curation. "If [cool restaurant] partners with Mingla, Mingla must know good spots."
- Perks give users a tangible reason to open the app before going out.
- It's free. Venues get customers, you get credibility.
- Creates a content flywheel: venue posts about Mingla → their followers discover you.

**Do NOT make this a deals/coupon app.** The perk is a cherry on top, not the value prop. Mingla is about discovery, not discounts. The moment you lead with "save 10% at restaurants," you're Groupon. You're dead.

#### Tier 5: Paid Acquisition (Cost: $$$ — only after organic validates)

**Do NOT spend money on ads until:**
1. You know which message resonates (organic tells you this)
2. Your retention is healthy (D7 > 40%, D30 > 20%)
3. Your unit economics work (LTV > 3x CAC)

**When ready:**
- **Meta (Instagram/Facebook):** Couples targeting, interest-based (date night, restaurants, experiences). Start with $20–$50/day. Test 5 creatives per week. Kill losers fast.
- **TikTok Ads:** Spark Ads (boost your own organic posts that performed well). Cheapest way to scale what's already working.
- **Google App Campaigns:** Capture high-intent searches ("date ideas," "things to do tonight"). Small budget, high conversion.
- **Apple Search Ads:** Bid on competitor terms and category terms. High intent, expensive but converts.

**Budget rule of thumb for launch:** $0 on paid until Month 3. Then $1,000–$3,000/month to test. Scale only when CAC < $5 and D30 retention > 20%.

### Distribution Channels NOT Worth Pursuing (Yet)

| Channel | Why not |
|---------|---------|
| **Podcasts** | Expensive, hard to measure, wrong format for a visual product |
| **Print/OOH** | Can't measure, can't target, burns cash |
| **PR/press** | Unpredictable timing, one-time spike, no sustained growth |
| **Partnerships with dating apps** | They see you as adjacent competition, won't promote you |
| **University campus marketing** | Your core user is 25–34 with disposable income, not 19-year-old students |

## 7. Launch Playbook

### The City-by-City Model (Non-Negotiable)

Mingla's value depends on **curation quality.** A half-curated city is worse than no city — it breaks trust on the first session. You must launch city by city.

### Pre-Launch (4–6 weeks before)

1. **Curate the pool.** Every place in the city categorized, price-tiered, verified. This is the product. If the pool is thin, the app feels empty.
2. **Recruit 10–20 venue partners.** Focus on places that are genuinely good, not just willing. Quality > quantity.
3. **Seed 15–30 micro-influencers.** Give them early access. Let them create content before launch day.
4. **Build a waitlist landing page.** City-specific: "Mingla is coming to [City]. Join the waitlist." Collect emails + phone numbers for launch day push.
5. **Create 20–30 pieces of organic content.** Bank them. You need a content runway for the first 2 weeks.

### Launch Week

1. **Day 1:** App goes live in the city. Push notification/email to waitlist. All seeded influencer content goes live simultaneously.
2. **Days 1–3:** Post 2–3x/day on all social channels. Respond to every comment. The algorithm rewards early engagement.
3. **Days 3–7:** Run a "Launch Week Challenge" — "Use Mingla for your date this weekend, post your experience, tag @mingla." Best post wins a free year of Mingla+.
4. **Day 7:** Share user-generated content from the challenge. Social proof > your own marketing.

### Post-Launch (Ongoing)

- **Weekly "Mingla Pick"** social post featuring a standout venue
- **Monthly content themes** aligned with seasons/holidays (Valentine's, summer, holiday season)
- **Continuous pool maintenance** — add new venues, remove closed ones, update categories
- **Monitor retention** — if D7 drops below 35%, diagnose before expanding to next city

### City Prioritization Framework

Launch where these three things overlap:

1. **High density of quality venues** (your pool needs to be rich)
2. **Strong couples/young professional population** (your users need to exist)
3. **You have personal knowledge or connections** (curation quality depends on local insight)

## 8. Metrics That Matter

### North Star Metric

**Weekly Active Sessions (WAS):** Number of unique sessions (solo or collaborative) where a user swiped at least 3 cards in the past 7 days.

Why: It captures both engagement (they opened the app and used it) and the collaboration flywheel (sessions = social planning).

### Funnel Metrics

| Stage | Metric | Healthy Target |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| **Acquisition** | Weekly installs | Growing 10%+ week-over-week |
| **Activation** | % who complete first swipe session within 24 hours | > 60% |
| **Retention** | D7 retention | > 40% |
| | D30 retention | > 20% |
| **Revenue** | Free → Mingla+ conversion rate | > 5% of MAU |
| | ARPU (average revenue per user) | > $1.50/mo blended |
| **Referral** | % of users who invite at least 1 friend | > 25% |
| | Viral coefficient (K-factor) | > 0.5 (each user brings 0.5 new users) |

### Metrics to Watch But Not Optimize For

- **Total downloads** — vanity metric if retention is bad
- **Time in app** — more time could mean engagement OR confusion
- **Number of places in pool** — quality matters more than quantity

### Metrics That Signal Trouble

- **D1 retention < 30%** — onboarding is broken or first session is underwhelming
- **Collab session creation < 15% of users** — your differentiator isn't landing
- **Trial → paid conversion < 3%** — value prop isn't clear or price is wrong
- **Referral rate < 10%** — the product isn't creating "tell a friend" moments

## 9. Risks & Things to Think About Now

### Risk 1: Cold Start / Empty City Problem

**The risk:** You launch in a city with a thin pool. User opens app, sees 15 mediocre places, never opens it again. You get one shot at a first impression.

**Mitigation:**
- Never launch a city until the pool has 150+ verified, categorized, high-quality places
- Manually curate the first 50 "hero" places that you'd personally recommend
- Better to delay a city launch than to launch thin

### Risk 2: "I Found My Spots" Churn

**The risk:** User discovers 10–15 great places in month 1–2, then feels like they've "beaten" the app. They know their spots now. They stop opening Mingla.

**Mitigation:**
- Continuous pool refresh — new places added monthly
- Seasonal/event-based content ("Summer rooftop series," "Holiday date ideas")
- "You haven't tried" nudges — surface places they haven't swiped yet
- This is your biggest long-term retention threat. Think about it constantly.

### Risk 3: The Yelp Comparison

**The risk:** Users (and investors, and press) say "so it's like Yelp but for dates?" and dismiss you.

**Mitigation:**
- Never let your marketing mention restaurant discovery. Lead with the *social planning* angle.
- Yelp is individual search. Mingla is collaborative discovery. Different verbs entirely.
- Your categories (Nature & Views, Picnic Park, Live Performance) are experiences, not cuisines. Lean into this.

### Risk 4: One Half of the Couple Churns

**The risk:** One partner downloads and loves it. The other never engages. The active partner loses motivation because collaboration requires two.

**Mitigation:**
- The onboarding for invited users (via pairing) must be frictionless — skip everything except auth
- Push notifications to the invited partner: "[Name] saved a spot for your next date — tap to see it"
- The invited experience should feel like a gift, not a chore

### Risk 5: Pricing Backlash

**The risk:** Users feel the free tier is a trap, or the paywall triggers negative App Store reviews.

**Mitigation:**
- Free tier MUST be genuinely useful (hence the restructure recommendation above)
- Never gate core functionality behind payment — gate expansion
- Respond to every negative review within 24 hours (App Store visibility algorithm rewards this)

### Risk 6: Platform Dependency

**The risk:** Google Maps API pricing changes, or Apple/Google take 30% of your revenue through IAP, crushing margins.

**Things to think about now:**
- Google Maps API costs scale with usage. Budget for this. Monitor per-user API cost.
- Apple/Google take 30% (15% if you qualify for small business program < $1M revenue). At $4.99/mo, you keep ~$3.50–$4.25 per subscriber.
- Consider web-based subscription purchase to avoid the 30% cut (Spotify model) — but this adds friction.
- Have a backup plan if Google Maps API costs spike: cache aggressively, consider alternative data sources.

### Risk 7: Seasonal Usage Patterns

**The risk:** Usage spikes in spring/summer (people go out more) and dips in winter (people stay home). Revenue becomes unpredictable.

**Mitigation:**
- Winter content: "cozy date ideas," indoor experiences, holiday planning
- Annual subscriptions smooth revenue (users pay upfront regardless of seasonal behavior)
- This is normal for lifestyle apps — plan for it, don't panic during dips

### Things You Must Decide NOW (Before Launch)

1. **Which city launches first?** And do you have 150+ quality places ready?
2. **Free tier scope.** Is pairing free or not? This decision shapes your entire growth model.
3. **Brand voice guidelines.** Write 10 example push notifications, 10 error messages, 10 social posts. This forces you to define the voice before scaling content.
4. **App Store page.** Screenshots, description, and keyword strategy must be ready before any marketing drives installs. This is your storefront — optimize it before driving traffic.
5. **Analytics infrastructure.** Are you tracking the metrics above? If not, you're flying blind. Set up Mixpanel/Amplitude/PostHog NOW. RevenueCat handles revenue metrics. You need funnel + retention tracking.
6. **Legal.** Privacy policy, terms of service, data handling (especially location data). Non-negotiable for App Store approval and user trust.
7. **Support channel.** When users have problems, where do they go? In-app chat? Email? A broken experience with no support path = 1-star review.

## 10. Competitive Landscape

### Direct Competitors (Small, Similar Concept)

| App | What they do | Mingla's advantage |
|-----|-------------|-------------------|
| **DateSpot** | Date idea generator | No real-time place data, no collaboration |
| **Duo** | Couples date planner | Limited cities, no friend groups |
| **Fever** | Experience booking | Event-focused (tickets), not everyday dates |

None of these have Mingla's combination of mood-based curation + price tiers + real-time collaboration.

### Indirect Competitors (Large, Adjacent)

| App | Threat level | Why Mingla wins |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| **Google Maps** | Medium | Overwhelming, no curation, no collaboration, no mood filtering |
| **Yelp** | Low | Review-focused, not experience-focused. Searching ≠ discovering. |
| **TripAdvisor** | Low | Tourist-focused, not local-focused |
| **Instagram "Saved"** | High | Many people save restaurant posts as a de facto "to-try" list. Unstructured, no collaboration, no categories — but habitual. |
| **The group chat** | Highest | This is your real competitor. "Let's just figure it out ourselves." Mingla must be faster and better than 5 friends texting links back and forth. |

### Competitive Moat (What's Defensible)

1. **Curated pool quality.** Anyone can pull from Google Maps API. Not everyone can categorize 13 ways + 4 price tiers + verify quality. This is manual work that compounds.
2. **Collaborative planning UX.** Building real-time sessions with pairing, boards, and proposals is non-trivial. Copycats need months to replicate.
3. **Local knowledge network.** Venue partnerships + user-generated signals (saves, swipes) create a local taste graph that improves over time.
4. **Brand identity in the "planning" moment.** If Mingla becomes the verb ("let's Mingla it"), that's the ultimate moat.
